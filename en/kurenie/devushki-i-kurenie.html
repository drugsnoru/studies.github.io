Girls and smoking, gender perspective


Smoking is a serious health and social problem worldwide. It is also the leading cause of preventable morbidity and mortality. More than 8 million people die from tobacco use worldwide each year. Half of people who smoke die from tobacco-related diseases, and half of those deaths occur in middle age. Even though smoking prevalence has historically been higher among men, in many countries there has been an increase in smoking among young women compared to young men.

An important body of evidence shows that women may be more sensitive than men to some of the harmful effects of smoking. Moreover, smoking in women carries the risk of gender-specific conditions such as premature menopause, fertility disorders, and pregnancy and newborn complications. Smoking prevention and cessation strategies must take a gender perspective into account in order to reduce the impact of tobacco use on women.

Epidemiology


Globally, 942 million men and 175 million girls aged 15 years and older are current smokers. Nearly three quarters of men who smoke daily live in countries with medium or high human development index (HDI), while half of women who smoke daily live in countries with very high HDI
