It is no secret that many smokers suffer from bad breath from time to time. Although heavy smokers are most often affected, even those who smoke sporadically may be unsuccessful enough to suffer from bad breath.

Alarmingly, studies show that smokers often experience "impairment of olfactory sensation." In short, you may not even realize that you are suffering from a smoker’s breath; and if you can smell your own breath, the problem may be much more serious than you think.

What causes bad breath from smokers?


There are three most common causes of breath odor among smokers, although they can often overlap and affect each other.

Tobacco chemicals

Not surprisingly, the most immediate cause of "smoker’s odor" is tobacco itself. Not only do the lungs retain some of this tobacco smoke even some time after the cigarette has gone out, but the chemicals remain in the mouth.

Dry mouth


A longer-term problem may be exposure to tobacco smoke on bacteria in the mouth. These sulfur-producing bacteria feed on proteins and produce chemicals known as volatile sulfur compounds (or VSCs).

Human saliva has a powerful ability to counteract the activity of oral bacteria due to its pH. This is why many dentists recommend using sugar-free chewing gum after meals, which stimulates saliva flow, thereby helping to fight harmful bacteria.

Smoke inhalation was found to have a "significant correlation" with dry mouth (3), meaning that smoking can lead to the growth of bacteria that cause halitosis.

Dry mouth not only affects breathing but has also been shown to be associated with other dental problems such as gingivitis (edema of the gum line) and tartar formation.

Oral hygiene problems


The third and final cause of breath odor in smokers can be (in some cases) related to poor oral hygiene. Studies have shown that smoking is often "associated with higher VSC values", likely as a result of two previous causes (5).

Persistent dry mouth can lead to tartar, plaque, and other related hygiene problems.

Although many smokers deny that smoking can cause oral health problems, effective oral care is more than just brushing teeth (which we will talk about shortly).

How to get rid of the smell from the smoker's mouth

Although the most obvious solution to the problem of bad breath after smoking is to give up this habit, we are well aware of how difficult it can be.

Fortunately, there are a number of other solutions that can be useful to eliminate the smell from the smoker's mouth.

Bad breath (halitosis)


Halitosis—or bad breath—may result from poor oral hygiene, but it is not always the case. It can also result from a number of health problems, including dry mouth, heartburn, or even disease of another part of the body. Treatment of halitosis depends on the underlying cause.

What is halitosis?

Halitosis is a medical term for bad breath. Every once in a while, people have a bad breath, especially after eating garlic, onions, or other spicy foods. But bad breath that doesn't go away (chronic halitosis) can mean that you have oral health problems or a condition that affects another part of the body.

Halitosis is a symptom of many different states. In other words, it's like a warning message from your body. Finding the root cause of halitosis is the first step to treating the problem.

How common is halitosis?


Halitosis is common and affects about 1 in 4 people worldwide. One study combining the results of 13 articles in medical journals found that halitosis affected about 31.8% of the population.
